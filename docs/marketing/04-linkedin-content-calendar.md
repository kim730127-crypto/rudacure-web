**Slide 2:**
> "TRPV1 is an ion channel — a tiny gated pore in cell membranes that opens in response to heat, capsaicin (chili pepper), and tissue damage."

**Slide 3:**
> "When TRPV1 opens, calcium and sodium flow into the neuron, triggering the pain signal that travels to your brain. Useful for survival — but problematic when chronic."

**Slide 4:**
> "Most existing drugs try to BLOCK TRPV1 directly. The problem? Blocking the channel also blocks your sense of heat — risking burns and skin temperature problems."

**Slide 5:**
> "RudaCure's approach is different. Instead of blocking the channel, RCI001 modulates the downstream inflammatory signal (Rac1/NLRP3) — silencing the chronic inflammation while keeping heat sensation intact."

## POST 3 — Industry POV (Week 1, Friday)

**Pillar:** Industry POV
**Account:** Dr. Yong-ho Kim Personal
**Visual:** Photo of Dr. Kim with simple quote graphic

```
The dry eye therapeutic landscape changed in 2025-2026.

Senju Pharmaceutical's Avarept (Motogivatrep) was approved in Japan as a first-in-class TRPV1 antagonist. Alcon launched a TRPM8 ion channel agonist via FDA in mid-2025. Sun Pharmaceutical's SJP-0132 is in clinical trials. The ion channel approach to dry eye is finally validated.

But here's what I tell investors who ask "Isn't RCI001 too late?":

Direct TRPV1 antagonists block the channel. They reduce some inflammation but they also blunt heat sensation — a known limitation that has slowed first-generation development for decades.

RCI001 takes a different path. It modulates the downstream Rac1/NLRP3 inflammatory cascade WITHOUT blocking the channel itself. We get the anti-inflammatory benefit without the thermosensation cost.

Late entry to a category usually means losing. But late entry with a meaningfully different mechanism — that's when biotech becomes most interesting.

We move RCI001 into Korea Phase 2 in H1 2026. I'd love to discuss with anyone working in this space.

— Yong-ho Kim, Founder & CEO, RudaCure
```
